Problem
A patient reports seeing asymmetric, comet-shaped streaks radiating from point light sources when viewing objects in their peripheral visual field through their new spectacle lenses. This is most characteristic of which aberration?
KEY DIAGNOSTIC FEATURES
Asymmetric blur pattern (not circular)
Comet-shaped appearance with directional tail
Occurs in peripheral vision (off-axis viewing)
Affects point sources of light
Understanding Coma

Coma is a monochromatic (non-color-related) aberration that specifically affects light rays entering a lens at an angle to the optical axis. The name comes from the characteristic "comet" shape of the blur pattern produced.

  • Off-axis aberration: Only occurs when viewing through peripheral lens areas, not through the optical center
  • Asymmetric blur: Unlike spherical aberration (circular blur), coma produces directional distortion
  • Zone-dependent: Rays through upper lens zones focus at different heights than lower zone rays
  • Increases with obliquity: Worsens as viewing angle increases from optical axis
Clinical Significance

In spectacle lenses, coma becomes noticeable when patients look through peripheral areas of the lens rather than the optical center. This explains why:

1. Proper optical center alignment is crucial for spectacle comfort

2. Patients should be instructed to move their head rather than just their eyes when possible

3. High-power lenses require more careful fitting and centration

4. Modern lens designs optimize base curves to minimize peripheral aberrations including coma

Answer: Coma
Why Other Answers Are Incorrect
✗ Spherical aberration

Spherical aberration affects on-axis rays and produces symmetric, circular blur patterns. It occurs because marginal rays (through lens periphery) and paraxial rays (near optical center) focus at different distances along the optical axis. This creates blur even when viewing through the lens center, unlike the patient's symptoms which occur only in peripheral vision. The blur from spherical aberration is radially symmetric with no directional component or comet shape.

✗ Chromatic aberration

Chromatic aberration results from wavelength-dependent refraction, causing different colors to focus at different points. This produces colored fringes around objects (typically red-blue or yellow-purple halos) rather than monochromatic comet-shaped streaks. The patient describes shape distortion without mentioning color separation, ruling out chromatic aberration as the primary cause.

✗ Astigmatism

Astigmatism produces elliptical or linear blur patterns due to different refractive powers in different meridians. While astigmatic blur is directional, it creates consistent elongation in a specific axis (horizontal, vertical, or oblique) rather than the progressive, smeared comet shape characteristic of coma. Astigmatic blur also affects both central and peripheral vision uniformly if uncorrected, whereas coma specifically manifests in off-axis viewing.

Summary

The combination of asymmetric blur, comet-shaped appearance, peripheral location, and point source distortion definitively indicates coma. The off-axis nature of the symptoms (only in peripheral visual field) is the key diagnostic feature that distinguishes coma from other aberrations.
