Problem
When viewing an off-axis point source through a spectacle lens, asymmetric blur with progressive directional smearing occurs. What is the primary optical cause of this phenomenon?
CORRECT ANSWER
Oblique rays through different lens zones experience varying magnification leading to differential lateral image displacement
Understanding the Mechanism

Coma arises from a fundamental property of oblique ray bundles: when light from an off-axis point enters a lens at an angle, different zones of the lens produce different lateral magnifications. This is not about different focal distances along the optical axis (which would be spherical aberration), but rather different lateral positions in the image plane.

Why Magnification Varies with Zone Height

For obliquely incident rays, the geometrical conditions are asymmetric across the lens aperture. A ray passing through the upper portion of the lens encounters different effective angles and optical path lengths compared to a ray through the lower portion, even though both originate from the same object point.

The result: rays through peripheral zones undergo greater lateral displacement than rays through central zones. Since displacement increases progressively from center to edge, the image of a point becomes smeared in one direction, creating the characteristic asymmetric blur pattern.

Clinical Implications

Understanding that coma results from magnification variation explains several clinical observations:

1. Aperture Dependence: Larger apertures mean rays pass through zones with greater variation in magnification, worsening coma. Stopping down reduces the effective aperture, limiting rays to zones with more similar magnification.

2. Base Curve Design: Spectacle lens manufacturers optimize base curves to minimize magnification variation across the lens, reducing coma for peripheral viewing.

3. Patient Instructions: Patients should be taught to view through the optical center where oblique incidence is minimized, reducing the magnification variation that causes coma.

4. Power Relationship: Higher power lenses create greater magnification differences between zones, explaining why coma is more problematic in high prescriptions.

Answer: Oblique rays through different lens zones experience varying magnification leading to differential lateral image displacement
Why Other Answers Are Incorrect
✗ "Marginal and paraxial rays focus at different axial distances creating depth-dependent blur"

This describes spherical aberration, not coma. In spherical aberration, the issue is longitudinal (axial) - rays focus at different distances along the optical axis. This creates symmetric, circular blur because the problem is rotationally symmetric around the axis. Spherical aberration occurs even for on-axis viewing. The question specifies asymmetric blur with directional smearing, which indicates lateral (not axial) displacement and requires off-axis conditions.

✗ "Different wavelengths refract at different angles producing wavelength-dependent image positions"

This describes chromatic aberration, which results from dispersion - the wavelength-dependent refractive index of optical materials. Chromatic aberration produces colored fringes (typically red-blue separation) rather than the monochromatic directional smearing characteristic of coma. Both longitudinal chromatic aberration (different focal distances for different wavelengths) and lateral chromatic aberration (different magnifications for different wavelengths) involve color separation, which is not mentioned in the question.

✗ "The lens surfaces have different radii of curvature in orthogonal meridians"

This describes astigmatism, where the lens has different powers in different meridians (typically along vertical and horizontal axes). Astigmatism creates two line foci at different distances, producing elliptical blur that can be oriented in various directions. However, astigmatic blur is determined by the meridional structure of the lens itself and affects both central and peripheral vision consistently. It does not produce the progressive, zone-dependent lateral displacement that characterizes coma. The blur pattern in astigmatism depends on which focal line the image plane intersects, not on varying magnification across lens zones.

Summary

Coma is fundamentally a lateral magnification problem, not an axial focusing problem. The varying magnification across different lens zones for obliquely incident rays causes different amounts of lateral displacement, creating progressive smearing. This distinguishes it from spherical aberration (axial displacement, symmetric), chromatic aberration (wavelength-dependent, colored), and astigmatism (meridional power differences, elliptical blur).
