Diffraction is at its maximum in small aperture systems and is therefore the most correct answer.

Radial astigmatism is a type of Seidel aberration that happens when the light passing through the edge of the lens (which is off-center from the pupil) gets bent more than the light passing through the center of the lens. This causes the light to form an oblong shape instead of a circular shape on the retina, which can make objects appear distorted or blurry. This effect is typically more prominent in wide aperture systems.

Coma is another Seidel aberration that causes light rays to be focused at different angles and positions within the pupil, resulting in images that are distorted and comet-shaped. Coma is typically more prominent in wide aperture systems.

Spherical aberration, another Seidel aberration, occurs when light rays passing through different portions of a lens or mirror are focused at different distances from the optical axis, resulting in blurred or distorted images. Spherical aberration is typically more prominent in wide aperture systems.

Depth of focus is the range of distances over which an object appears to be in focus in an image. However, in this scenario, the limiting factor in the resolution of the eye is not the depth of focus, but rather the diffraction caused by the small pupil size.

Optics (Geometrical)AberrationsSpherical aberrationTo minimize spherical aberration when using a plano-convex lens to focus distant light, which orientation is preferred?Convex surface toward the distant object; Flat surface toward the distant object; Either orientation produces equal aberration; Orientation depends on the lens powerConvex surface toward the distant objectSpherical aberration is minimized when refraction is distributed as evenly as possible between the two lens surfaces. For a plano-convex lens focusing parallel light from a distant source, the convex surface should face the incoming light. This orientation allows both surfaces to share the bending of rays, reducing the excessive refraction that occurs when all bending happens at a single surface.

When the flat surface faces the distant object, the first surface performs no refraction while the second surface must do all the work. This unequal distribution increases spherical aberration because peripheral rays are bent too sharply at the curved surface.

The principle extends to other lens forms as well. For any lens focusing distant light, the more curved surface should generally face the object. Conversely, when forming an image at a distant location, the more curved surface should face the image. This best-form orientation is a practical consideration in optical system design.

Optics (Geometrical)AmetropiaAberrations - SphericalWhich best explains why visual acuity may decrease when a patient is dilated, despite accurate refractive correction?Marginal rays focus at a different distance than paraxial rays; Different wavelengths of light focus at different distances; Off-axis object points produce asymmetric images; The retinal image of a flat object is curvedMarginal rays focus at a different distance than paraxial raysSpherical aberration occurs because rays passing through different zones of a lens focus at different points along the optical axis. For a plus lens, marginal rays passing through the peripheral zones are refracted more strongly and focus closer to the lens than paraxial rays passing near the optical center. This creates a blur circle rather than a sharp focal point. When pupils dilate, peripheral lens zones contribute to image formation, increasing the impact of spherical aberration.

With dilation, aberrated marginal rays reach the retina and focus at a different plane, degrading image quality despite accurate refractive correction. This explains why patients with dilated pupils may experience reduced acuity and why some individuals see better in bright light when smaller pupils limit the eye to paraxial rays. In the human eye, the cornea produces positive spherical aberration while the young crystalline lens contributes negative spherical aberration that partially compensates. This balance shifts with age as lens shape changes, which contributes to increased higher-order aberrations in older patients.

While present in the eye, chromatic aberration is not specifically worsened by pupil dilation in the manner described. Off-axis object points producing asymmetric images describes coma, an aberration affecting oblique rays that creates characteristic comet-shaped blur patterns pointing toward or away from the optical axis. A curved retinal image of a flat object describes curvature of field, where the focal surface is curved rather than flat. This aberration affects the shape of the image plane rather than causing the on-axis blur associated with dilated pupils.
